Introduction to Reinforcement Learning (RL)

Why RL Exists

Reinforcement Learning is necessary when we need a system to make a series of decisions to achieve a long-term goal, especially in complex, dynamic environments where the rules are not always known beforehand. Unlike typical programming where you give the computer explicit instructions, in RL, the computer learns through trial and error—like training a puppy with rewards. The goal is to maximize the total reward accumulated over time, not just the immediate reward.

How RL Solves New Problems

The Limitation of Supervised Learning

Supervised Learning (like recognizing cats in pictures) requires a labeled dataset—it only works if you provide the *exact* correct answer for every situation. It cannot figure out a complex sequence of actions, such as how to fly a plane or win a chess game, because there isn't one "correct" move at every step; success depends on future moves.

The Limitation of Unsupervised Learning

Unsupervised Learning (like grouping similar customers) finds patterns in data without labels. While useful for organization, it has no concept of "goals" or "success." It can't learn how to drive a car because it has no mechanism to know that crashing is bad (negative reward) and staying on the road is good (positive reward). RL fills this gap by introducing the concepts of time, interaction, and reward signals.

Key Glossary

(Displayed as 10 cards, each containing the three parts of the glossary entry.)

Agent
The learner and decision-maker.
Example: The self-driving car itself.
Environment
Everything the agent interacts with.
Example: The road, traffic, and weather.
State
A specific situation in the environment.
Example: Car's speed, location, and nearby objects.
Action
A move the agent can make.
Example: Accelerate, brake, or steer left.
Reward
A feedback signal (positive or negative).
Example: +10 for reaching the goal; -5 for hitting a curb.
Policy
The agent's strategy, mapping states to actions.
Example: The rule: "If speed is X and traffic is Y, then brake."
Value Function
Predicts how much total future reward is expected.
Example: The long-term goodness of a state.
Q-Value
Predicts the value of taking a specific action in a specific state.
Example: How good is it to 'steer left' right now?
Episode
A single, complete run from start to finish.
Example: One entire trip from home to school.
Exploration/Exploitation
The dilemma of trying new things vs. sticking to known good things.
Example: Trying a new street vs. taking the usual, known route.
